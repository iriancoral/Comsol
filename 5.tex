\section{Conclusion}
\label{ch:conclusion}
% ============================================================

In this report the design and optimisation of a car rear-window defroster using has been presented using coupled
multiphysics finite element modelling in COMSOL Multiphysics~6.2. In order to complete the design of the heating trace 
pattern six simulations have been analyzed each with its own respective optimization part.  Each stage narrowed 
the design space using the results of the previous one, 
producing a final configuration that satisfies the dew-point, defrost-time, stress, and obscuration requirements 
simultaneously.

Four main conclusions can be drawn from the study. First, the number of heating traces is by far the most 
influential design parameter as increasing the trace count from 12 to 20 at \SI{12}{\volt} raises the 
minimum steady-state temperature $T_\text{min}$ by more than \SI{6}{\kelvin}. In contrast raising the 
voltage from \SI{12}{\volt} to \SI{14.5}{\volt} at fourteen traces gives an increase of only \SI{1.2}{\kelvin}. 

Second, out of all the materials the Pd--Ag alloy ($\sigma = \SI{2.3e6}{\siemens\per\metre}$) 
is a clear winner in terms of heat generation and distribution, reaching the dew point with fourteen 

traces at \SI{12}{\volt} where Nichrome~V needs eighteen to twenty. However, bulk-conductivity materials 
such as silver and gold overheat too much in this geometry and cannot be used for the trace pattern optimization. 

\textcolor{red}{Sebtain pls confirm}Third, the stress analysis confirms that thermal expansion mismatch 
between the traces and the glass does not pose a structural risk, with safety 
factors exceeding $3\times$ for both shortlisted materials. 
Fourth, geometry optimisation proves to be as meaningful as material or 
electrical optimisation. With the application of the Chebyshev trace spacing (\Cref{eq:chebyshev}) with exponent 
$p = 1.3$ is shown that the defrost time is halved from \SI{12}{\minute} to \SI{6}{\minute} and the 
$T_\text{min}$ is raised by \SI{6.8}{\kelvin} with zero additional cost which resulted to be the optimal design.

More importantly, the recommended design is not tuned to a 
single favourable operating temperature, to check for validity five weather scenarios are tested in Sim~6 --- ranging ambient 
temperatures from \SIrange{-20}{10}{\celsius}, external convection coefficients from 
\SIrange{10}{40}{\watt\per\metre\squared\per\kelvin} and physically realistic dew points 
\textcolor{red}{Sebtain do you know this source?}
\cite{lawrence2005} --- all defrost within \SI{8}{\minute} with a minimum steady-state 
margin of \SI{+5.4}{\kelvin} above the dew point, confirming robustness across the expected 
operating envelope.

The simulation methodology itself with coupling Electric Currents, Heat Transfer, and Solid 
Mechanics through parametric sweeps over material properties, geometry, and operating 
conditions provides a systematic and reusable framework for optimization of trace pattern optimization. 
Furthermore, the result is supported by mesh convergence studies, back-of-envelope 
analytical estimates (agreeing to within 0.03\%) and boundary conditions. 
To conclude the key insight is that the Chebyshev spacing 
rule offers a mathematically principled formula based on a single-parameter that can be 
applied to any rectangular heated-glass application. Future work should extend the model 
to three-dimensional glass, validate the simulation results against a physical 
prototype and investigate the effect of varying interior convection ($h_\text{int}$) 
under different car air conditioning states.
