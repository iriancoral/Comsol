% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Rear window condensation and frost is a common problem in automotive vehicles, particularly during colder months where the temperature of the glass surface drops below the dew point of the surrounding air \cite{lawrence2005}. When this happens, moisture from the cabin air condenses on the inner surface of the glass, which significantly reduce the driver's rear visibility and creates a safety hazard \cite{sayer1999}. To addresses this issue, most modern vehicles are equipped with electrically heated rear window defrosters that uses thin resistive traces embedded on the glass to raise it's surface temperature above the dew point.

\subsubsection{Problem Statement}

The aim of this study is to design and optimise a resistive rear window defroster system for a standard automotive application using finite element multiphysics modelling in COMSOL Multiphysics 6.2. Specifically, the study seeks to answers the following questions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item What is the optimal number of heating traces and operating voltage which is needed to ensure the entire glass surface temperature exceeds the dew point?
    \item Which trace material from the available candidates (sourced from the CES EduPack database \cite{edupack}) provides the best balance between thermal performance and structural integrity?
    \item How long does it takes for the defroster to fully clear the window from an initial frosted condition, and can this be improved through non-uniform trace spacing?
    \item Is the final design robust enough to performs adequately across a range of realistic weather conditions?
\end{enumerate}

The defroster must raises the entire glass surface temperature above the dew point to eliminate condensation and frost, while remaining within safe thermal and mechanical stress limits. The design is developed iteratively through a series of simulations of increasing complexity, starting from a baseline model and progressively refining the configuration through parametric sweeps, material selection, stress analysis, trace spacing optimisation, and weather robustness testing.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Problem Description}
\label{sec:problem-description}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Rear window defrosters works by passing electric current through thin conductive traces which are deposited on the inner surface of the glass \cite{ruhl2016}. The Joule heating produced in the traces then conducts into the glass, raising it's temperature above the dew point \cite{koshlak2024}. The main design challenge is to achieve a sufficiently uniform heating across the entire window, using minimum number of traces at a voltage that is compatible with standard \SI{12}{\volt} automotive electrical system \cite{bosch2014}.

The design must satisfy three main criterias:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Thermal:} The minimum surface temperature of the glass must exceeds the dew point temperature ($T_\text{dew} = \SI{283.15}{\kelvin}$, i.e.\ \SI{10}{\degreeCelsius}) \cite{lawrence2005}.
    \item \textbf{Structural:} Thermal stresses from the differential expansion between trace material and the glass must remain below the material yield strengths \cite{ruhl2016}.
    \item \textbf{Transient:} The window should defrost within a reasonable amount of time (target: $\leq \SI{15}{\minute}$) \cite{prudenziati2012}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Geometry IRIAN}
\label{sec:geometry}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The model is a two-dimensional representation of a flat rear window. The geometry consists of three components:

\begin{itemize}
    \item A rectangular glass pane of dimensions $L_\text{glass} \times H_\text{glass}$ = \SI{1.5}{\metre} $\times$ \SI{0.8}{\metre}
    \item Horizontal conductive heating traces spanning the full width of the glass, created using a linear array
    \item Two vertical bus bars (left and right edges) connecting all traces to the power supply
\end{itemize}

The number of traces ($n_\text{traces}$) is a key design variable, swept from 8 to 22 in the parametric studies to find the optimal trace pattern. The traces are uniformly spaced across the glass height. \Cref{fig:geometry} shows the COMSOL geometry for the baseline configuration.

Since the model is two-dimensional with out-of-plane thickness, an effective 2D electrical conductivity is used for the traces:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_\text{eff} = \sigma_\text{trace} \cdot \frac{t_\text{trace}}{t_\text{glass}}
    \label{eq:sigma-eff}
\end{equation}
This accounts for the fact that the thin conductive traces occupy only a fraction of the out-of-plane glass thickness. The trace dimensions used here is consistent with typical screen-printed defroster traces reported in the literature \cite{prudenziati2012}.

\subsection{Boundary Conditions IRIAN}
\label{sec:boundary-conditions}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The boundary conditions set for the base simulation represent a cold winter morning scenario, 
where the outside of the glass is exposed to 
ambient air and the interior is facing the inside of the car. The values are the base values for
all simulations and listed in \Cref{tab:bcs}.

\begin{table}[H]

        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}

The dew point temperature $T_\text{dew}$ is estimated from the cabin air conditions using the Magnus approximation \cite{lawrence2005}:
\begin{equation}
    T_\text{dew} = \frac{b \, \gamma(T,\mathrm{RH})}{a - \gamma(T,\mathrm{RH})},
    \qquad
    \gamma(T,\mathrm{RH}) = \ln\!\left(\frac{\mathrm{RH}}{100}\right) + \frac{a\,T}{b + T}
    \label{eq:dew-point}
\end{equation}
with Magnus constants $a = 17.625$ and $b = \SI{243.04}{\degreeCelsius}$, and $T$ in \si{\degreeCelsius}. For a cabin at $T_\text{cabin} = \SI{20}{\degreeCelsius}$ and a representative relative humidity $\mathrm{RH} = \SI{50}{\percent}$:
\begin{equation*}
    \gamma = \ln(0.50) + \frac{17.625 \cdot 20}{243.04 + 20} \approx 0.647
    \quad\Rightarrow\quad
    T_\text{dew} \approx \frac{243.04 \cdot 0.647}{17.625 - 0.647} \approx \SI{9.3}{\degreeCelsius}.
\end{equation*}
This is rounded up to $T_\text{dew} = \SI{10}{\degreeCelsius}$ (\SI{283.15}{\kelvin}) to provide a small safety margin against higher cabin humidity (e.g.\ from passengers breathing).

Furthermore a electric potential $V_\text{in}$ is applied on the left bus bar, and ground ($V = 0$) on the right bus bar.
The electric current physics are defined only on the trace and bus bar
domains and not on the glass.

\subsection{Physics Coupling and Governing Equations}
\label{sec:governing-equations}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The model involves three coupled physics: Electric Currents, Heat Transfer in Solids, and Solid Mechanics. Their coupling is illustrated in \Cref{fig:coupling-diagram}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{physics_coupling_diagram.png}
    \caption{Physics coupling in the multiphysics model. Joule heating from Electric Currents drives Heat Transfer, which in turn drives thermal expansion in Solid Mechanics.}
    \label{fig:coupling-diagram}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Electric Currents.} The electric field in the conductive traces is governed by current conservation:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla \cdot \mathbf{J} = 0
    \label{eq:current-conservation}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{J} = \sigma_\text{eff} \mathbf{E}$ is the current density and $\mathbf{E} = -\nabla V$ is the electric field.

\textbf{Heat Transfer in Solids.} The heat equation in the glass domain is:
\begin{equation}
    \rho C_p \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot (k \nabla T) + Q_e + Q_\text{conv}
    \label{eq:heat-equation}
\end{equation}
where $Q_e = \mathbf{J} \cdot \mathbf{E}$ is the resistive (Joule) heating from the Electromagnetic Heating multiphysics coupling, and $Q_\text{conv}$ is the convective heat source from \Cref{eq:heat-source}.

For stationary studies, the left-hand side is zero and the equation reduces to:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla \cdot (k \nabla T) + Q_e + Q_\text{conv} = 0
    \label{eq:heat-stationary}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Solid Mechanics.} The mechanical equilibrium is described by:
\begin{equation}
    \nabla \cdot \mathbf{S} + \mathbf{F}_v = 0
    \label{eq:solid-mechanics}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{S}$ is the Cauchy stress tensor. Thermal expansion introduces an initial strain:
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_\text{th} = \alpha (T - T_\text{ref}) \mathbf{I}
    \label{eq:thermal-expansion}
\end{equation}
with reference temperature $T_\text{ref} = T_\text{amb} = \SI{275.15}{\kelvin}$.
