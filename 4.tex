\section{Effect of Trace Material Conductivity}
\label{sec:material-discussion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------
SEBTAIN?

A key finding of this study is the strong dependence of defroster performance on the trace electrical conductivity. The Joule heating power per unit length of trace scales as:
\begin{equation}
    P \propto \frac{V_\text{in}^2 \cdot \sigma_\text{eff}}{L_\text{trace}}
    \label{eq:power-scaling}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma_\text{eff}$ is the effective 2D conductivity from \Cref{eq:sigma-eff}. This explains why:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Materials with very high conductivity (Silver: $5.75 \times 10^7$, Gold: $4.2 \times 10^7$\,S/m) generate excessive heating, reaching temperatures of 500--600\,K that would crack the glass.
    \item Materials with very low conductivity (Carbon ink: $10^4$\,S/m) generate negligible heating and cannot defrost the window.
    \item The optimal conductivity range for this geometry and voltage is $\sim 10^5$--$10^6$\,S/m, which corresponds to thick-film paste materials (Pd--Ag alloy, Nichrome~V).
\end{itemize}

\section{Trace Pattern Optimisation}
\label{sec:pattern-results}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Two approaches to non-uniform trace spacing were investigated: a discrete zoned layout (Approach~A, Sim~4) and a continuous Chebyshev distribution (Approach~B, Sim~5). Both use 20~traces of Pd--Ag alloy at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$ to allow direct comparison with the uniform baseline.

The zoned configuration places 6~traces in the top \SI{200}{\milli\metre}, 8~traces in the middle \SI{400}{\milli\metre}, and 6~traces in the bottom \SI{200}{\milli\metre}. This reduces the edge gap from \SI{38.1}{\milli\metre} (uniform) to \SI{28.6}{\milli\metre} while widening the centre gap to \SI{44.4}{\milli\metre}.

The time-dependent simulation shows that this configuration defrosts in \SI{11}{\minute} (vs.\ \SI{12}{\minute} for uniform) and reaches a steady-state $T_\text{min}$ of \SI{289.3}{\kelvin} (vs.\ \SI{288.2}{\kelvin}). The improvement is modest: \SI{1}{\minute} faster defrost and \SI{1.1}{\kelvin} higher $T_\text{min}$.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Strengths}

With Chebyshev spacing ($p = 1.3$), the final design (Pd--Ag alloy, 20~traces, \SI{12}{\volt}) achieves a steady-state $T_\text{min}$ of \SI{295.0}{\kelvin} --- \SI{12}{\kelvin} above the dew point --- and defrosts in only \SI{6}{\minute}, compared to \SI{12}{\minute} with uniform spacing. This improvement comes purely from trace placement, at zero additional cost. The thermal stresses remain within safe limits (safety factor $\sim 3.1 \times$) and the design operates at nominal \SI{12}{\volt} battery voltage, meaning it works even with the engine off. The parametric approach --- sweeping material, trace count, voltage, and spacing exponent --- provides a comprehensive design map that allows easy trade-off analysis for different requirements. The multiphysics coupling between Electric Currents, Heat Transfer, and Solid Mechanics captures the essential physics of the defroster operation, and the mesh convergence study confirms that results are mesh-independent for the recommended configurations. The boundary conditions ($h_\text{ext}$, $h_\text{int}$) are supported by published experimental data~\cite{hozejowska2019}, and the sequential solve strategy for the stress analysis overcomes the COMSOL~6.2 spatial frame limitation while still capturing the thermal stress state.

\subsection{Limitations, Future Work, and Broader Implications}

Several simplifications limit the fidelity of the current model. The 2D flat geometry does not capture the curvature of a real rear window, which affects heat flux distribution and stress concentrations; a 3D simulation would be more realistic. The fixed constraint at the glass edges overestimates the peak stress, and modelling the rubber seal as an elastic support would yield more accurate values. The heat transfer coefficients are held constant, whereas in reality $h_\text{ext}$ depends on vehicle speed (forced convection) and $h_\text{int}$ on cabin HVAC settings; a weather condition sweep varying $T_\text{amb}$, $T_\text{dew}$, and $h_\text{ext}$ across climate scenarios would test design robustness. Furthermore, the EduPack material properties for the Pd--Ag alloy correspond to bulk material, while screen-printed thick-film pastes may behave differently, making experimental validation with a physical prototype recommended. Finally, the model does not account for electrical contact resistance between traces and bus bars, which may reduce the effective heating power.

The Chebyshev spacing result has broader implications beyond this specific defroster. The key insight is that \textbf{non-uniform spatial distribution of heat sources}, optimised using a single continuous parameter, can be as effective as changing materials or operating conditions. This principle applies to any distributed heating or cooling problem where temperature uniformity is the objective.

In the context of automotive defrosters, the Chebyshev approach could be directly applied to front windshield defrosters (where driver visibility constraints are even more stringent) or to heated mirrors. The $p$-parameter provides a simple manufacturing specification: a single number that fully defines the trace layout for any given window height and trace count.

Furthermore, the observation that bulk-conductivity materials (Silver, Gold) overheat catastrophically in this geometry has a practical consequence: screen-printed thick-film pastes are not merely a cost-saving alternative to bulk metals --- they are \emph{functionally necessary} for the correct thermal behaviour. This finding is consistent with the industrial practice of using Pd--Ag and Ag paste formulations with controlled conductivity \cite{prudenziati2012}.
