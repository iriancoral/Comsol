\section{Effect of Trace Material Conductivity}
\label{sec:material-discussion}
% ------------------------------------------------------------
SEBTAIN?

A key finding of this study is the strong dependence of defroster performance on the trace electrical conductivity. The Joule heating power per unit length of trace scales as:
\begin{equation}
    P \propto \frac{V_\text{in}^2 \cdot \sigma_\text{eff}}{L_\text{trace}}
    \label{eq:power-scaling}
\end{equation}

where $\sigma_\text{eff}$ is the effective 2D conductivity from \Cref{eq:sigma-eff}. This explains why:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Materials with very high conductivity (Silver: $5.75 \times 10^7$, Gold: $4.2 \times 10^7$\,S/m) generate excessive heating, reaching temperatures of 500--600\,K that would crack the glass.
    \item Materials with very low conductivity (Carbon ink: $10^4$\,S/m) generate negligible heating and cannot defrost the window.
    \item The optimal conductivity range for this geometry and voltage is $\sim 10^5$--$10^6$\,S/m, which corresponds to thick-film paste materials (Pd--Ag alloy, Nichrome~V).
\end{itemize}

\section{Trace Pattern Optimisation}
\label{sec:pattern-results}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Two approaches to non-uniform trace spacing were analyzed: a discrete zoned layout (Approach~A, Sim~4) and a 
continuous Chebyshev distribution (Approach~B, Sim~5). Both use 20~traces of Pd--Ag alloy at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$ 
to make sure the simulations are directly comparable to the uniform baseline (Sim~3).

The zoned configuration places 6~traces in the top \SI{200}{\milli\metre}, 8~traces in the middle \SI{400}{\milli\metre} 
and 6~traces in the bottom \SI{200}{\milli\metre}. This reduces the edge gap from \SI{38.1}{\milli\metre} (uniform) to \SI{28.6}{\milli\metre} 
while widening the centre gap to \SI{44.4}{\milli\metre}.

The time-dependent simulation shows that this configuration defrosts in \SI{11}{\minute} (vs.\ \SI{12}{\minute} for uniform) and reaches a steady-state $T_\text{min}$ of \SI{289.3}{\kelvin} (vs.\ \SI{288.2}{\kelvin}). The improvement is modest: \SI{1}{\minute} faster defrost and \SI{1.1}{\kelvin} higher $T_\text{min}$.

\subsection{Limitations, Future Work, and Broader Implications}

In order to complete the analyses approximations have been made that lower 
the accuracy of the present analysis. 
The use of 2D planar geometry is not accurate as it fails to consider 
the actual curved shape of the rear 
windshield, which influences the heat flux and stress distribution; a 3D 
analysis would have been more realistic. The application of fixed boundary conditions 
at the glass edges is an overestimation of the maximum stress levels as realistically they would
be lower. Using elastic 
springs instead of rigid supports to model the rubber gasket would produce more realistic 
results, since a rear window is supported by an elastic seal and the car frame. Heat transfer 
coefficients are kept fixed throughout the analysis, however, in reality, $h_\text{ext}$ 
varies with vehicle speed due to convection, while $h_\text{int}$ depends on the 
cabin airflow conditions set by the internal air conditioning system. Using
variable climatic conditions and varying $T_\text{amb}$, $T_\text{dew}$, and $h_\text{ext}$ would 
give more realistic results. 
Furthermore, the material properties used were referenced from the EduPack database for bulk materials. 
The thick-film paste used in screen printing may have different properties due to the manufacturing process and microstructure, 
which could also affect the heating performance.


The Chebyshev spacing result has broader implications beyond the rear window heating trace pattern. The key insight is that 
non-uniform spatial distribution of heat sources, optimised using a single continuous parameter, can 
be as effective as changing materials or operating conditions. This principle applies to any distributed heating 
or cooling problem where temperature uniformity has to be achieved.
