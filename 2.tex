% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Simulation 1 --- Baseline Stationary Model}
\label{sec:sim1}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The first simulation establishes the baseline model with the default configuration: 12 traces of silver paste ($\sigma = \SI{2e6}{\siemens\per\metre}$) at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$. A stationary study couples Electric Currents and Heat Transfer via Electromagnetic Heating.

The purpose of Sim~1 is to verify that:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The voltage gradient across the traces is correct (12\,V at the left bus bar, 0\,V at the right)
    \item The temperature distribution is physically realistic (in the range 270--320\,K)
    \item The cold spots are located at the expected positions (top and bottom edges farthest from traces)
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{Sim1 —voltage distribution.png}
    \hfill
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{Sim1 —temperature distribution.png}
    \caption{Sim~1 baseline results at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$, $n_\text{traces} = 12$, silver paste. (a)~Electric potential distribution on the traces and bus bars. (b)~Temperature distribution across the glass surface.}
    \label{fig:sim1-results}
\end{figure}

The baseline results show a surface-average temperature of \SI{297.13}{\kelvin} (above the dew point) but a surface-minimum temperature of \SI{282.12}{\kelvin} (below the dew point). The cold spots are at the top and bottom edges of the glass where trace coverage does not extend. This motivates the parametric sweeps in the following simulations.

\section{Simulation 2c --- Stress Analysis}
\label{sec:sim2c}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Sim~2c evaluates the thermal stresses for the two shortlisted materials (Pd--Ag alloy and Nichrome~V). Solid Mechanics and Thermal Expansion are re-enabled, and a sequential solve is used:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Step 1:} Electric Currents + Heat Transfer (stationary)
    \item \textbf{Step 2:} Solid Mechanics with Thermal Expansion (stationary, using Step~1 temperatures)
\end{itemize}

This sequential approach avoids the spatial frame conflict (``dvol\_spatial undefined'') encountered when solving all physics simultaneously in COMSOL~6.2.

\section{Simulation 3 --- Transient Defrost Study}
\label{sec:sim3}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Sim~3 evaluates how long each configuration takes to defrost the window from the initial frosted condition ($T_\text{init} = \SI{278.15}{\kelvin}$) to the point where $T_\text{min} \geq T_\text{dew}$.

A time-dependent study is used with output times from 0 to \SI{3600}{\second} in \SI{60}{\second} increments. The Electric Currents equation is set to stationary form (the electric field equilibrates in microseconds compared to the thermal time scale of minutes). Solid Mechanics is disabled for computational efficiency.

The sweep covers: 2~materials $\times$ 5~trace counts (14, 16, 18, 20, 22) $\times$ 2~voltages (12, \SI{14.5}{\volt}) = 20~runs. The mesh was refined to Fine (189,192~domain elements) for accuracy.

Based on these results, \textbf{Pd--Ag alloy with $n_\text{traces} = 20$ at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$ is selected as the recommended design}. The key reasons are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Battery-only operation:} A nominal automotive 12\,V system operates directly from the battery without the engine running \cite{bosch2014}. This is critical for a rear defroster, which must function during a cold start before the engine---and therefore the alternator---is active. Voltages of \SI{13.5}{\volt} and \SI{14.5}{\volt} are only available when the alternator is charging, requiring the engine to be running.
    \item \textbf{Acceptable defrost time:} At \SI{12}{\volt}, 20~traces defrosts the window in \SI{12}{\minute} --- well within a practical warm-up period.
    \item \textbf{Adequate thermal margin:} The steady-state $T_\text{min} = \SI{288.2}{\kelvin}$ is \SI{5.05}{\kelvin} above the dew point, providing a comfortable margin against condensation re-formation.
    \item \textbf{Diminishing returns at 22~traces:} Increasing to 22~traces reduces defrost time by only 2~minutes (10 vs.\ 12~min at \SI{12}{\volt}) at the cost of higher material use and increased trace density.
\end{itemize}

An alternative configuration of 22~traces at \SI{14.5}{\volt} achieves defrost in \SI{8}{\minute} but requires the engine to be running and is therefore only suitable as a secondary high-performance mode.


% ------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Simulation 4 --- Trace Pattern Optimisation}
\label{sec:sim4}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

After establishing the optimal material (Pd--Ag alloy) and operating conditions in the previous simulations, Sim~4 investigates the effect of trace layout on the temperature uniformity across the glass surface. The baseline simulations revealed that cold spots form at the top and bottom edges of the glass, farthest from any heating trace. A natural mitigation strategy is to concentrate more traces near these edges.

The glass is divided into three horizontal zones with independent trace densities:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Top zone:} $H_\text{top} = \SI{200}{\milli\metre}$, $n_\text{top} = 6$ traces
    \item \textbf{Middle zone:} $H_\text{mid} = \SI{400}{\milli\metre}$, $n_\text{mid} = 8$ traces
    \item \textbf{Bottom zone:} $H_\text{bot} = \SI{200}{\milli\metre}$, $n_\text{bot} = 6$ traces
\end{itemize}
This gives a total of 20~traces (same as the recommended uniform design) but with denser spacing in the edge zones. The edge gap is \SI{28.6}{\milli\metre} (vs.\ \SI{38.1}{\milli\metre} uniform), and the centre gap is \SI{44.4}{\milli\metre}.

Zone parameters were added to Global Definitions and each zone was created as a separate Rectangle + Array in the geometry, with spacing $H_\text{zone}/(n_\text{zone}+1)$. The old uniform-spacing geometry (Array~1 + Rectangle~2) was disabled.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{Sim4 geometry zoned 6 - 8 - 6 layout.png}
    \hfill
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{Sim4 temperature distribution — zoned 6-8-6 t= 660.png}
    \caption{Zoned trace layout (Approach~A). (a)~Geometry with 6/8/6 distribution across top/middle/bottom zones. (b)~Temperature distribution at $T = T_\text{dew}$ showing reduced cold spots at the edges.}

The study was changed from stationary to time dependent with output range \texttt{range(0,60,3600)}, i.e.\ every \SI{60}{\second} for \SI{60}{\minute}. The Electric Currents equation form was set to Stationary (as in Sim~3) since the electric field equilibrates instantaneously compared to the thermal time scale. The initial temperature was set to $T_\text{init} = \SI{278.15}{\kelvin}$.

The zoned approach was not pursued further because the continuous Chebyshev spacing (Sim~5) proved far more effective, as discussed in \Cref{sec:sim5}.


% ============================================================
\section{Simulation 5 --- Chebyshev Trace Spacing Optimisation}
\label{sec:sim5}
% ============================================================

While the zoned approach (Sim~4) provides a discrete improvement, a mathematically elegant alternative is to use a continuous spacing function that naturally clusters traces near the edges. Chebyshev node spacing, derived from the roots of Chebyshev polynomials, is well known in numerical analysis for minimising interpolation error by placing points near interval boundaries. This property is analogous to the defroster design goal of concentrating heating where it is most needed.

\subsection{Spacing Formula and Implementation}

The vertical position $y_i$ of the $i$-th trace ($i = 1, \ldots, 20$) is given by:
\begin{equation}
    y_i = \frac{H_\text{glass}}{2} \left(1 - \text{sign}\!\left(\cos\frac{i\pi}{21}\right) \left|\cos\frac{i\pi}{21}\right|^p\right)
    \label{eq:chebyshev}
\end{equation}
where $p$ is a clustering exponent that controls how aggressively the traces are pushed toward the edges:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $p < 1$: More uniform than standard Chebyshev (less edge clustering)
    \item $p = 1$: Standard Chebyshev distribution
    \item $p > 1$: More aggressive edge clustering
\end{itemize}

The $\text{sign}(\cdot)|\cdot|^p$ formulation preserves the symmetry of the cosine distribution for all values of $p$, including non-integer exponents.

Twenty individual rectangles were created in the geometry, each with a unique $y$-position computed from \Cref{eq:chebyshev}. The exponent $p$ was defined as a global parameter, enabling a parametric sweep. All other model settings (material: Pd--Ag alloy, $\sigma_\text{eff}$ correction, time-dependent study, boundary conditions) were identical to Sim~4.

\subsection{Results and Optimum}

Three values of $p$ were tested first to establish the feasibility of the approach:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $p = 1.0$ (standard Chebyshev): \textbf{defrost in \SI{7}{\minute}}, steady-state $T_\text{min} = \SI{292.6}{\kelvin}$
    \item $p = 0.85$: \textbf{never reaches dew point} --- steady-state $T_\text{min} = \SI{282.06}{\kelvin}$
    \item $p = 0.7$: \textbf{never reaches dew point} --- steady-state $T_\text{min} = \SI{282.06}{\kelvin}$
\end{itemize}

The $p = 1.0$ result is dramatic: defrost time is reduced from \SI{12}{\minute} (uniform) to \SI{7}{\minute} --- a 42\% improvement with exactly the same number of traces, same material, and same voltage. However, reducing $p$ below 1.0 removes too much edge clustering and the cold spots return.

To find the optimal exponent, a parametric sweep was performed over $p \in \{0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95, 1.0, 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4, 1.5, 1.6, 1.7, 1.8, 1.9, 2.0, 3.0\}$. All runs use 20~traces of Pd--Ag alloy at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$. The trace $y$-positions were expressed parametrically in terms of $p$, so COMSOL automatically updates the geometry for each sweep value.

The results are presented in \Cref{tab:sim5-p-sweep} and plotted in \Cref{fig:sim5-p-sweep}.

The optimum is clearly at $p = 1.3$: it achieves the highest minimum temperature at the 6-minute mark (\SI{283.20}{\kelvin}) and the highest steady-state $T_\text{min}$ (\SI{295.0}{\kelvin}). The physical interpretation is:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$p < 1.0$:} Insufficient edge clustering. The edges remain cold, and the centre is over-heated with closely spaced traces --- reproducing the original problem.
    \item \textbf{$p = 1.0$--$1.3$:} Increasing $p$ progressively moves more traces to the edges, heating the cold spots more effectively. The centre gap widens but the glass thermal conductivity is sufficient to bridge it.
    \item \textbf{$p > 1.3$:} Over-clustering at edges. The centre becomes a cold spot because the traces are too far apart. At $p = 2.0$ the cosine-squared creates three clusters (two edges plus centre), and at $p = 3.0$ the clustering is too sharp for the window height.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Practical Considerations}

An important practical constraint for rear window trace patterns is driver visibility. Per the SAE~J216 standard, obscuration of the rear vision area should not exceed 5\% in the primary zone \cite{sayer1999}. For 20~traces with $w_\text{trace} = \SI{1.7}{\milli\metre}$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Obscuration} = \frac{n_\text{traces} \times w_\text{trace}}{H_\text{glass}} = \frac{20 \times 1.7}{800} = 4.25\%
    \label{eq:obscuration}
\end{equation}
This is well within the 5\% limit regardless of the spacing pattern, since the total trace area is unchanged. The Chebyshev spacing only redistributes the same number of traces, so visibility is not affected.

Adding vertical traces between the horizontal ones was considered to create a grid pattern. However, analysis showed this would be ineffective: since all parallel horizontal traces are at the same voltage at any given $x$-position, vertical connectors between them would have zero potential difference ($\Delta V = 0$) and carry no current. Variable-width traces could create voltage differences enabling vertical current flow, but this adds significant manufacturing complexity without clear benefit over optimising the horizontal spacing alone.

All previous simulations used a fixed ambient condition: $T_\text{amb} = \SI{2}{\celsius}$, $h_\text{ext} = \SI{15}{\watt\per\metre\squared\per\kelvin}$, and $T_\text{dew} = \SI{10}{\celsius}$. All five scenarios pass the defrost criterion with positive margin. Key observations are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Faster defrost under harsher convection:} Scenarios with high $h_\text{ext}$ (freezing rain: 40, extreme cold: 30) defrost in under 4~minutes. The high convective coefficient drives a steeper thermal gradient, which accelerates the initial transient even though the steady-state temperature is lower.
    \item \textbf{Tightest margin in freezing rain (Scenario~5):} With $h_\text{ext} = \SI{40}{\watt\per\metre\squared\per\kelvin}$ and a high convective heat loss, the steady-state $T_\text{min}$ is only \SI{5.4}{\kelvin} above the dew point. This is the worst-case scenario for sustained condensation prevention.
    \item \textbf{Largest margin in cool autumn (Scenario~4):} Low convective loss and mild ambient conditions result in a \SI{21.4}{\kelvin} margin --- more than sufficient.
    \item \textbf{Robustness across $\Delta T_\text{amb} = \SI{30}{\kelvin}$:} The design passes all scenarios spanning a \SI{30}{\kelvin} ambient temperature range (\SIrange{-20}{10}{\celsius}), demonstrating robustness to real-world variability.
\end{itemize}
