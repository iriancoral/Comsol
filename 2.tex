\section{Simulation 1 --- Baseline Stationary Model}
\label{sec:sim1}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

% The first simulation establishes the baseline model with the default configuration: 12 traces of silver paste ($\sigma = \SI{2e6}{\siemens\per\metre}$) at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$. A stationary study couples Electric Currents and Heat Transfer via Electromagnetic Heating.

% The purpose of Sim~1 is to verify that:
% \begin{itemize}
%     \item The voltage gradient across the traces is correct (12\,V at the left bus bar, 0\,V at the right)
%     \item The temperature distribution is physically realistic (in the range 270--320\,K)
%     \item The cold spots are located at the expected positions (top and bottom edges farthest from traces)
% \end{itemize}


The first simulation uses the default model setup: 12 silver-paste traces ($\sigma = \SI{2e6}{\siemens\per\metre}$) with an input voltage of $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$. A stationary study is used, combining Electric Currents and Heat Transfer through Electromagnetic Heating.

Sim~1 is mainly used as a first check that the model behaves as expected and the firstly assumed amount of traces are somewhat realistic. The voltage should drop from $12 V$ at the left bus bar to $0 V$ at the right bus bar. The temperature should stay within a realistic range of about $270$ to $320K$, and the coldest regions should appear near the top and bottom edges, where there are no traces to enclose the space.
Traces were not placed at the top and bottom edges of the glass, as placing traces there would trivially reduce cold spots since more traces equal more heat. The purpose of this study is to optimize the trace
distribution pattern within a fixed heated region, showing that the performance can be increased significantly through pattern optimization.

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{Sim1 —voltage distribution.png}
    \hfill
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{Sim1 —temperature distribution.png}
    \caption{Sim~1 baseline results at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$, $n_\text{traces} = 12$, silver paste. (a)~Electric potential distribution on the traces and bus bars. (b)~Temperature distribution across the glass surface.}
    \label{fig:sim1-results}
\end{figure}

The baseline results show a surface-average temperature of \SI{297.13}{\kelvin} (above the dew point) but a surface-minimum temperature of \SI{282.12}{\kelvin} (below the dew point). The cold spots are at the top and bottom edges of the glass where trace coverage does not extend. These values are realistic and now it is possible to start looking at further optimization.

\section{Simulation 2 --- Voltage and Trace Count Sweeps}
\label{sec:sim2}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Sim~2b studies how temperature changes with input voltage  
and the number of traces in order to first find the optimal number of traces. Important to state is that these first simulations are stationary studies, so they do not give any information about how long it takes to defrost the window, but they do give information about the minimum temperature that can be reached for each configuration.  

Sim~2 then examines how the minimum glass  
temperature depends on voltage and trace count,  
both separately and in combination.

A parametric sweep of $V_\text{in}$ from \SI{8}{\volt} to \SI{16}{\volt} (9~values) is performed at $n_\text{traces} = 12$. Even at \SI{16}{\volt}, the minimum temperature (\SI{283.09}{\kelvin}) barely reaches the dew point, indicating that voltage alone cannot solve the problem with 12~traces.

A parametric sweep of $n_\text{traces}$ from 8 to 22 is performed at $V_\text{in} = \SI{12}{\volt}$. Results are shown in \Cref{fig:sim2-trace-sweep}.

\section{Simulation 2c --- Stress Analysis}
\label{sec:sim2c}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Sim~2c evaluates the thermal stresses for the two shortlisted materials (Pd--Ag alloy and Nichrome~V). Solid Mechanics and Thermal Expansion are re-enabled, and a sequential solve is used:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Step 1:} Electric Currents + Heat Transfer (stationary)
    \item \textbf{Step 2:} Solid Mechanics with Thermal Expansion (stationary, using Step~1 temperatures)
\end{itemize}

This sequential approach avoids the spatial frame conflict (``dvol\_spatial undefined'') encountered when solving all physics simultaneously in COMSOL~6.2.

\section{Simulation 3 --- Transient Defrost Study}
\label{sec:sim3}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Sim~3 evaluates how long each configuration takes to defrost the window from the initial frosted condition ($T_\text{init} = \SI{278.15}{\kelvin}$) to the point where $T_\text{min} \geq T_\text{dew}$.

A time-dependent study is used with output times from 0 to \SI{3600}{\second} in \SI{60}{\second} increments. The Electric Currents equation is set to stationary form (the electric field equilibrates in microseconds compared to the thermal time scale of minutes). Solid Mechanics is disabled for computational efficiency.

The sweep covers: 2~materials $\times$ 5~trace counts (14, 16, 18, 20, 22) $\times$ 2~voltages (12, \SI{14.5}{\volt}) = 20~runs. The mesh was refined to Fine (189,192~domain elements) for accuracy.

\section{Simulation 4 --- Trace Pattern Optimisation}
\label{sec:sim4}
% ------------------------------------------------------------
Now that the optimal material (Pd--Ag alloy) has been determined with its operating conditions, 
it is possible to look at the optimal trace pattern which is the scope of this project. 
The optimal trace pattern is one where after heating heats up the glass
as uniformly as possible in the shortest time possible. Although an obvious way to improve 
heating performance would be to increase both the number of traces and the applied voltage, 
this lies outside the scope of the project, since the aim is to optimize the pattern 
itself instead of overall input parameters

Sim~4 investigates the effect of trace layout on the temperature uniformity 
across the glass surface. The baseline simulations revealed that cold spots form at the top and 
bottom edges of the glass, farthest from any heating trace. Therefore a logical first strategy would 
be to increase the density of traces neaar the edges to improve the heating. 

This has been done for the first simulation and the glass was divided into three horizontal 
zones with independent trace densities \cref{fig:sim4-zoned}. Zone parameters were added to Global Definitions and each zone was created as a separate Rectangle + Array in the geometry, with spacing $H_\text{zone}/(n_\text{zone}+1)$.
:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Top zone:} $H_\text{top} = \SI{200}{\milli\metre}$, $n_\text{top} = 6$ traces
    \item \textbf{Middle zone:} $H_\text{mid} = \SI{400}{\milli\metre}$, $n_\text{mid} = 8$ traces
    \item \textbf{Bottom zone:} $H_\text{bot} = \SI{200}{\milli\metre}$, $n_\text{bot} = 6$ traces
\end{itemize}
% This keeps the same 20~traces but with denser 
% spacing in the edge zones. The edge gap is \SI{28.6}{\milli\metre} 
% (vs.\ \SI{38.1}{\milli\metre} uniform), and the centre gap is \SI{44.4}{\milli\metre}.


\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{Sim4 geometry zoned 6 - 8 - 6 layout.png}
    \hfill
    \includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{Sim4 temperature distribution — zoned 6-8-6.png}
    \caption{Zoned trace layout (Approach~A). (a)~Geometry with 6/8/6 distribution across top/middle/bottom zones. (b)~Steady-state temperature distribution showing reduced cold spots at the edges.}

The study was changed from stationary to time dependent with output range \texttt{range(0,60,3600)}, 
i.e.\ every \SI{60}{\second} for \SI{60}{\minute}, the 6/8/6 zoned configuration reached $T_\text{dew}$ (\SI{283.15}{\kelvin}) at $t = \SI{660}{\second} = \SI{11}{\minute}$. The Electric Currents equation was solved in stationary form
(as in Sim~3), because the electric field reaches equilibrium much faster than the temperature changes.
The initial temperature was set to $T_\text{init} = \SI{278.15}{\kelvin}$.
The zoned approach was not analysed further because the continuous Chebyshev spacing (Sim~5) turned out 
to be more effective, as discussed in \Cref{sec:sim5}.

\section{Simulation 5 --- Chebyshev Trace Spacing Optimisation}
\label{sec:sim5}
% ============================================================

While the zoned approach (Sim~4) shows improvement in the heating for the outer edges it is still not optimal and it is based 
on personal intuition of the distribution for the traces with no real mathematical backing. A mathematical alternative for the trace spacing is to 
use a continuous spacing function that clusters traces near the edges. Chebyshev node spacing, 
derived from the roots of Chebyshev polynomials, is known in numerical analysis for minimising interpolation 
error by placing points near interval boundaries. This property can be used for the window is design goal of 
putting heating where it is most needed.

\subsection{Spacing Formula and Implementation}

The vertical position $y_i$ of the $i$-th trace ($i = 1, \ldots, 20$) is given by:
\begin{equation}
    y_i = \frac{H_\text{glass}}{2} \left(1 - \text{sign}\!\left(\cos\frac{i\pi}{21}\right) \left|\cos\frac{i\pi}{21}\right|^p\right)
    \label{eq:chebyshev}
\end{equation}

The inner cosine term $\cos\!\bigl(\frac{i\pi}{n_\text{traces}+1}\bigr)$ produces Chebyshev-type nodes between $-1$ and $+1$ 
that are inherently denser near the extremes~\cite{trefethen2013approximation}. The outer factor 
$\frac{H_\text{glass}}{2}(1-\cdot\,)$ maps these values onto the full glass height. The exponent $p$
shapes the distribution: for $p>1$, the trace clustering increases at the centre of the glass and decreases at the edges,
 for $p<1$, the centre gap widens and the traces at the edge increase and eventually leading to uniform spacing as $p\to 0$.

Because raising a negative number to a non-integer power is 
impossible, the $\text{sign}(\cdot)|\cdot|^p$ formulation separates sign and magnitude, preserving symmetry for all real~$p$.

In COMSOL, the traces were positioned at the $y_i$ computed from \Cref{eq:chebyshev}. The exponent $p$ was 
defined as a global parameter so that it could be used in a parametric sweep and it became possible to 
automatically regenerate the geometry and re-solve for each value of~$p$. All other model settings (material: Pd--Ag alloy, $\sigma_\text{eff}$ correction, time-dependent 
study, boundary conditions) were kept identical to Sim~4.

    \hfill

\subsection{Results and Optimum}

Three values of $p$ were tested first to establish the feasibility of the approach:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $p = 1.0$ (standard Chebyshev): \textbf{defrost in \SI{7}{\minute}}, steady-state $T_\text{min} = \SI{292.6}{\kelvin}$
    \item $p = 0.85$: \textbf{never reaches dew point} --- steady-state $T_\text{min} = \SI{282.06}{\kelvin}$
    \item $p = 0.7$: \textbf{never reaches dew point} --- steady-state $T_\text{min} = \SI{282.06}{\kelvin}$
\end{itemize}

Already for $p = 1.0$ an improvement can be noticed with a time reduction
from \SI{12}{\minute} (uniform) to \SI{7}{\minute}, a 42\% improvement 
with the same number of traces, same material, and same voltage. 
However, reducing $p$ below 1.0 removes too much edge clustering 
and the cold spots return.

To find the optimal exponent, a parametric sweep was performed for various 
values for p, the results are presented in \Cref{tab:sim5-p-sweep} and plotted in \Cref{fig:sim5-p-sweep}.

The optimum is clearly at $p = 1.3$, see \cref{fig:sim5-p-sweep}: it achieves the highest minimum

increasing or decreasing the p-value will lead to optimal behaviour. 
The same pattern will repeat itself for higher values of p but the wavelength
will increase and the clusters are not visible anymore for the 
respective window height.

\subsection{Practical Considerations}


An important practical constraint for rear window trace patterns is driver visibility. Per the SAE~J216 standard, 
obscuration of the rear vision area should not exceed 5\% in the primary zone \cite{sayer1999}. 
For 20~traces with $w_\text{trace} = \SI{1.7}{\milli\metre}$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Obscuration} = \frac{n_\text{traces} \times w_\text{trace}}{H_\text{glass}} = \frac{20 \times 1.7}{800} = 4.25\%
    \label{eq:obscuration}
\end{equation}
This is well within the 5\% limit regardless of the spacing pattern, since the total trace area is unchanged. 
The Chebyshev spacing only redistributes the same number of traces, so visibility is not affected. Additionally, 
UNECE Regulation No.~43 requires a minimum light transmittance of 70\% for safety glazing in vehicles~\cite{unece_r43}, 
which is easily satisfied given the low obscuration ratio.

As a further form of optimization it was considered to add vertical traced connecting the horizontal ones.

Adding vertical traces between the horizontal ones was considered to create a grid pattern. 
However, analysis showed this would be ineffective: since all parallel horizontal traces are at 
the same voltage at any given $x$-position, vertical connectors between them would have zero 
potential difference ($\Delta V = 0$) and carry no current. Variable-width traces could create 
voltage differences enabling vertical current flow, but this adds significant manufacturing 
complexity without clear benefit over optimising the horizontal spacing alone.
